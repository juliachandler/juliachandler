Grizzly Bear Forage Project · 2023–2025

The Berry Project

How logging, site, and climate shape the berry-producing shrubs that sustain grizzly bear habitat in British Columbia.

About the project

Black huckleberry (Vaccinium membranaceum) and buffaloberry/soapberry (Shepherdia canadensis) are among the most important food sources for grizzly bears in British Columbia's interior forests, and they carry deep cultural and nutritional significance for Indigenous communities. This government grant–funded project, completed in November 2025, modeled how forest structure, logging practices, site conditions, and climate influence the cover and berry abundance of these shrubs — and what that means for wildlife habitat and ecosystem function.

The work applied statistical modeling and geospatial analysis to ecological datasets, producing management-relevant guidance and two manuscripts currently in press.

Funding & role

The research was commissioned by the Province of British Columbia (Ministry of Forests — Lands, Natural Resource Operations and Rural Development), with Dr. Garth Mowat, the Province's Large Carnivore Specialist, as Ministry Representative. The contracts were awarded to Hamilton Ecological Consulting (Evelyn Hamilton). Dr. Julia Chandler was the quantitative ecologist subcontracted to compile the underlying data from government records, conduct the analyses, and write the reports and extension notes.
